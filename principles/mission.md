# Mission

## Why I Build Software

What is most gratifying to me in this work is knowing that someone is using
something I made with my intelligence and my hands; that it makes a task
easier or allows the person to complete it more efficiently.

An employer or a client makes a project possible and deserves honest,
competent work. But the purpose I hold onto while building is the person at
the end of the chain: the person using the system in daily life, whose work,
time or access to information is changed by what I deliver.

I write software to create bridges and shorten paths.

## A Small Library System

Around twenty years ago, I built a small system for the library of an
educational institution. I defined a code structure for books that allowed the
library to arrange copies on shelves by affinity: category, work and copy. I
also built an ASP 3 page on the local network so students in the laboratory
could search not only for books, but inside their indexes, and see whether a
copy was available or when it was expected back.

It was a small system. Its importance did not come from scale or technical
fashion. It created a bridge between an institution, its books and its
students. Each student who found material more easily received a concrete
benefit from engineering work.

That remains a useful measure of professional satisfaction: the accumulated
number of people who could do something more easily, reliably or safely
because a system existed and was cared for.

## Consequences For Engineering

This mission has practical consequences:

- A feature is incomplete if it exists technically but is difficult for its
  intended user to find, understand or trust.
- A small application deserves clear data handling, dependable behavior and
  usable documentation when people rely on it.
- Performance, availability and accessible interaction matter because delay,
  outage and exclusion are experienced by people, not by architecture
  diagrams.
- A technical shortcut is not harmless when it transfers recurring work,
  confusion or risk to users or maintainers.
- Success is not measured only by delivery; it includes whether the software
  continues to serve its purpose responsibly.

## Professional Boundary

Being oriented toward users does not mean ignoring legitimate business needs,
budgets, deadlines or the judgment of colleagues. Software is produced inside
real organizations with competing constraints. It means that, when discussing
tradeoffs, the effect on the person who uses or depends on the system remains
visible and is not treated as an afterthought.

The rest of this book translates that position into daily engineering
practice.
