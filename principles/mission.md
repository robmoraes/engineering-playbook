# Mission

## Why I Build Software

The deepest satisfaction I find in programming is not that a program runs, or
that a difficult implementation is complete. It is the moment when work made
with thought and care becomes useful in another person's day: a search becomes
possible, a repeated task becomes lighter, an answer becomes more reliable, or
someone reaches what they needed with less difficulty.

An employer or a client makes a project possible and deserves honest,
competent work. But a system ultimately meets human lives through the people
who use it, depend on it or use it to help someone else. Their time,
attention, confidence and access to information give software its practical
meaning.

I write software to shorten paths between people and worthwhile outcomes. The
gratification is quiet but lasting: knowing that something I built performed a
small, real service in the world.

## Software As A Bridge

Software can also be a bridge.

A small automation can return hours of life. <br>
A well-conceived system can reduce daily friction.<br>
A clear interface can lessen operational anxiety. <br>
An integration can bring people and processes closer together.<br>

In the end, software engineering is also about shortening paths.

## A Small Library System

In 2002, soon after graduating, I built one of my first programs for the
library of an educational institution. The library already had a legacy
system: it issued an auto-incremented number that was printed, attached to
each book and used as the prevailing order on the shelves. That identified
items, but did little to place related works together.

I introduced a three-part numeric classification: category, work and copy. A
book could be positioned near others on the same subject, and a new edition or
additional copy of a work could be placed immediately after its peers. The
change was simple to state, but tangible in use: the physical collection began
to express affinity rather than only order of arrival.

The system provided screens for recording books and their indexes, and allowed
keyword searches through that indexed content. It also supported loans and
returns, reported overdue items and indicated expected return dates. The
librarian used this information to locate relevant material and inform people
interested in a borrowed work when it might become available.

I also built an ASP 3 page with the idea of making consultation available from
the laboratories. It was not put into service there. The actual result was
more modest and more honest: access still passed through the librarian. Yet
the benefit of the delivered system was real. It strengthened her ability to
guide students through the collection, answer questions and connect a person
with a book that could help them learn.

That experience remains important to me because it gives a human scale to the
profession. A system does not need a vast audience or fashionable technology
to justify pride in the work. Sometimes satisfaction comes from improving one
person's work so that she can better serve many others, and from knowing that
the care placed in structure, search and information reached people in a
practical way.

## Consequences For Engineering

This understanding of satisfaction has practical consequences:

- A feature is incomplete if it exists technically but is difficult for its
  intended user to find, understand or trust.
- A user may be the professional who mediates a service for others; improving
  that person's work can improve many people's access and experience.
- A small application deserves clear data handling, dependable behavior and
  usable documentation when people rely on it.
- Performance, availability and accessible interaction matter because delay,
  outage and exclusion are experienced by people, not by architecture
  diagrams.
- A technical shortcut is not harmless when it transfers recurring work,
  confusion or risk to users or maintainers.
- Success is not measured only by delivery; it includes whether the software
  continues to serve its purpose responsibly.

## Professional Boundary

Being oriented toward users does not mean ignoring legitimate business needs,
budgets, deadlines or the judgment of colleagues. Software is produced inside
real organizations with competing constraints. It means that, when discussing
tradeoffs, the effect on the person who uses or depends on the system remains
visible and is not treated as an afterthought. It also means describing our
own work honestly: an unrealized interface is not a delivered benefit, while a
less visible improvement in someone's daily service is still worth
recognizing.

The rest of this book translates that position into daily engineering
practice.
